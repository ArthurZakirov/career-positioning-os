# Value Grammar

This reference captures reusable principles for generating and communicating value across titles, thumbnails, articles, videos, repositories, products, services, job posts, and offers.

## Core Model

A person pays attention when the next unit of information appears to improve their situation more than the alternative use of their attention.

Practical equation:

```text
perceived value =
  desired outcome
  x likelihood of outcome
  x novelty / scarcity
  x receiver fit
  / time delay
  / effort, risk, complexity, and opportunity cost
```

The unit being judged can be a title, thumbnail, opening sentence, section header, product card, repository preview, job ad, sales page, or full product.

## Valuable Information Unit

Use this structure for any claim:

```text
Outcome + Evidence + Information Container
```

Examples of information containers:

- formula;
- framework;
- map;
- checklist;
- system;
- machine;
- matrix;
- decision tree;
- iceberg;
- mechanism;
- rule;
- principles;
- mistakes;
- myths;
- secrets;
- steps.

Containers increase believability because they imply the information is organized, reusable, and not merely a personal opinion.

## Receiver Fit

Value is not absolute. It depends on the receiver's situation and identity.

Increase receiver fit by naming:

- identity: engineers, founders, managers, students, creators, operators;
- situation: stuck, under deadline, scaling, hiring, learning, debugging, launching;
- awareness level: unaware, symptom-aware, problem-aware, solution-aware, skeptical;
- current alternative: what they would otherwise keep doing.

Specificity beats generic advice when the receiver recognizes themselves.

## Outcome Angles

Use one primary angle per value unit:

- `Avoid Pain`: avoid mistake, trap, rejection, wasted time, lost money, missed opportunity, failure, embarrassment, risk.
- `Get Pleasure/Gain`: make money, save time, gain status, increase confidence, improve health, win, get approval, build capability.
- `Reduce Effort`: simpler, faster, less confusing, lower-risk, fewer steps, no special tool, no prior expertise.
- `Shorten Time Delay`: now, today, in one week, before deadline, first 30 seconds, first version.
- `Improve Tradeoff`: get the upside without the expected downside.

Pain avoidance and risk reduction usually create stronger immediate attention than benefit promises.

## Curiosity Gap

Curiosity appears when the receiver can see that an answer exists but does not yet know the missing link.

Reliable patterns:

- known effect, unknown cause;
- familiar problem, surprising root cause;
- obvious answer rejected;
- two known facts with an unclear relationship;
- counterintuitive result;
- unlikely actor succeeds;
- specific number without the mechanism;
- named framework without its explanation.

Do not close the gap in the title if the goal is the click. Reveal enough to make the promise credible, not enough to remove the need to inspect.

## Believability Signals

Use the strongest honest proof available:

- observable before/after;
- specific number;
- event that happened;
- screenshot or artifact;
- social proof;
- expert or institution;
- external reference;
- story;
- logical argument;
- mechanism explanation;
- demo;
- constrained promise.

If proof is weak, reduce the promise. If the promise is strong, show proof early.

## Novelty and Supply/Demand

A true idea may still feel low-value if the receiver has heard it many times. Increase perceived scarcity by showing what is new:

- fresh mechanism;
- uncommon source;
- unusual combination;
- new context;
- hidden tradeoff;
- overlooked failure mode;
- specific niche adaptation;
- surprising visual representation.

Avoid novelty words without real novelty. The concept must become more inspectable, not just louder.

## Title Patterns

Use titles to communicate the highest useful abstraction of the content.

Patterns:

- `How to [desired outcome] without [expected cost]`
- `[Number] [container] that help [receiver] [outcome]`
- `The [container] behind [surprising result]`
- `Why [common solution] fails when [specific situation]`
- `Stop [costly behavior]. Do [mechanism] instead.`
- `[Known problem] is not caused by [obvious cause]. It is caused by [curiosity gap].`
- `I analyzed [evidence set]. Here is the pattern.`
- `The [matrix/map/system] that makes [confusing domain] legible`

Avoid:

- vague topic labels;
- broad life advice;
- titles that require prior trust;
- titles that reveal the whole answer;
- titles with no receiver, outcome, proof, or tension.

## Thumbnail Component Patterns

Treat thumbnails as value components, not decoration.

Useful components:

- `Before/After`: current pain on one side, desired outcome on the other.
- `Mechanism Object`: formula, matrix, pipeline, stack, map, machine, loop, decision tree.
- `Proof Artifact`: screenshot, metric, chart, visual receipt, visible output.
- `Threat Marker`: warning, red flag, hidden trap, broken path, expensive mistake.
- `Opportunity Marker`: unlocked path, green check, shortcut, leverage point.
- `Receiver Avatar`: the person or role affected by the problem.
- `Enemy/Constraint`: the obstacle, bottleneck, deadline, old belief, or wrong method.
- `Transformation Arrow`: movement from A to B.
- `Contrast Pair`: old way vs new way, obvious answer vs hidden answer.
- `Zoomed Detail`: one small part of a larger system that implies there is more to inspect.

Thumbnail text should usually be shorter than the title. It should not repeat the title verbatim. It should add contrast, proof, or mechanism.

## Title and Thumbnail Pairing

Strong pairings:

- title names the promise; thumbnail shows the mechanism;
- title names the pain; thumbnail shows the before/after;
- title names the result; thumbnail shows proof;
- title names the mystery; thumbnail shows the missing object;
- title names the receiver; thumbnail shows their situation.

Weak pairings:

- both say the same words;
- both are abstract;
- thumbnail is a generic illustration;
- title promises a result but thumbnail shows no proof or mechanism;
- visual includes too many concepts.

## Sequential Communication

For content behind the click, use attention loops:

```text
Situation -> Complication -> Question -> Answer promise
```

Then prove the answer:

```text
Claim -> Evidence -> Explanation -> Action
```

For longer pieces, structure the argument as a tree:

```text
Main claim
  -> subclaim 1
  -> subclaim 2
  -> subclaim 3
```

Each node should contain a value claim, not just a category label. A section titled `The System That Prevents Rework` is stronger than `System`.

## Objection Order

When trying to create belief, answer objections in this order:

1. Does this work in general?
2. Does this work for someone like me?
3. Can I make it work?
4. Will external constraints stop me?
5. What exactly do I do next?

Do not over-explain the recommendation before the receiver believes the outcome is desirable and plausible.

## Ethical Constraint

This framework should increase clarity and honest value perception. Do not fabricate proof, fake scarcity, hide material tradeoffs, exploit fear disconnected from the actual offer, or turn a weak product into a misleading promise.
