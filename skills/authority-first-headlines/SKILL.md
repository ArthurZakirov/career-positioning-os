# Authority-First Headlines Skill

## Purpose

Rewrite titles, headlines, repository descriptions, homepage hero lines, LinkedIn headlines, article titles, and section openers so they lead with credible authority before explaining the content.

Use this when a title sounds like a generic description instead of a reason to care.

## Core Pattern

Authority -> Outcome -> Mechanism -> Reader Promise

Examples of authority:

- A measurable result
- A recognized institution
- A senior audience
- A hard operating context
- A respected company, university, publication, or framework
- A concrete proof point such as "used by 20+ engineers"
- A rare achievement, ranking, certification, or adoption signal

## Rewrite Rules

1. Lead with proof before explanation.
   - Weak: "A tool for setting up AI development environments."
   - Stronger: "The AI workstation setup method adapted from senior engineering workflows."
2. Prefer specific authority over vague authority.
   - Weak: "Trusted by professionals."
   - Stronger: "Used by 20+ engineers and technical leaders."
3. Borrowed authority must be truthful and traceable.
   - Acceptable: "Adapted from practices learned from a senior engineering lead, then customized for my own workflow."
   - Not acceptable: "Google's official workflow" unless it actually is official.
4. Do not overclaim ownership of institutional authority.
   - Say "inspired by," "adapted from," "used in," "learned from," or "based on public guidance from" when appropriate.
   - Avoid implying endorsement.
5. Add curiosity when possible.
   - Weak: "How to prioritize automation."
   - Stronger: "The 770-hour automation opportunity hidden behind a 41-hour roadmap."

## Headline Templates

- `[Authority proof] + [result] + [reader promise]`
- `[Recognized source/framework] + [practical application]`
- `[Unexpected contrast] + [outcome] + [how-to promise]`
- `[Senior audience adopted X] + Here's how it works`
- `[Hard constraint] + [surprising result] + [repeatable method]`

## Checklist

- Does the title answer "why should I trust this?"
- Does it contain a concrete proof point if available?
- Does it avoid false endorsement?
- Does it make the reader curious?
- Does it imply a useful method, not just a finished artifact?
